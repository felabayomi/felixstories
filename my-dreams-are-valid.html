Episode One: The Return

Felix Adewale returned home with one suitcase, two unpaid invoices, and a phone full of messages he did not have the strength to answer.

From the outside, he still looked like a man who had done things. People remembered the businesses, the ideas, the talks, the big plans, the rooms where he had spoken with confidence. They remembered the version of him who could make the future sound possible.

They did not see the other version.

The version who sat in the back seat of the taxi and stared through the window like the town had become a mirror. Every street showed him something he had once promised himself. Every familiar corner asked the same quiet question.

So what happened to all your dreams?

Felix looked down at his hands. They were steady, but he did not feel steady. He had built things in technology, wellness, conservation, spirituality, service, and creativity. He had worked hard. He had believed deeply. He had trusted people. He had lost money. He had lost sleep. He had lost relationships. And somewhere along the way, he had started losing the part of himself that knew how to hope without fear.

The taxi stopped in front of an old community hall.

The building looked tired. One window was cracked. The paint was peeling. The front step had weeds growing through it. Years ago, children had danced there during holiday programs. Women had held meetings there. Men had argued politics there. People had celebrated birthdays, mourned losses, planned weddings, and borrowed chairs.

Now the place stood quiet, as if it had been waiting for someone to remember it.

Felix paid the driver and stepped out.

He did not know why he walked toward the door. Maybe because broken things recognize each other. Maybe because the hall looked exactly how he felt: still standing, but not sure why.

Inside, dust covered the floor. Old posters curled on the wall. A stack of chairs leaned in one corner like tired people. Felix stood in the middle of the room and listened to the silence.

For the first time in months, he did not try to sound wise. He did not pray a perfect prayer. He did not make a plan.

He only whispered, "I do not know if my dreams still matter."

The words filled the empty hall.

And because the room did not laugh at him, because it did not ask him to explain, because it simply held the truth, Felix began to cry.

When he was done, he found an old broom and started sweeping.

By evening, he had made a small circle with six chairs. On each chair, he placed a sheet of paper and a pencil. On the wall, he taped one handwritten sentence.

My Dreams Are Valid.

The next morning, one woman stood at the door. She was holding a handbag with both hands, like it was the only thing keeping her together.

"Is this the place," she asked, "where people can talk about dreams that died?"

Felix looked at the six empty chairs. Then he looked at the woman.

"Yes," he said. "But maybe we do not have to call them dead yet."
